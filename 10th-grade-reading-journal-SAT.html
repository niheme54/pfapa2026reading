⚖️Prompt 2 · Contrasting Characters as ArgumentSAT: Analysis
Ralph and Jack represent opposing philosophies of leadership and human nature. Write a focused paragraph arguing which character Golding presents as more truthful about humanity. Use two quotes — one from each character — as evidence.
💡 SAT Skill: Comparative character analysis is a core SAT question type. Practice making a clear claim and backing it with specific, contrasting evidence.

💡 SAT Skill: Tracing a symbol across a text is the same skill as tracking a word's meaning shift across a passage — a direct SAT Reading question type.

🌾Prompt 1 · Foreshadowing & StructureSAT: Reading
Steinbeck uses foreshadowing so precisely that the ending feels inevitable. Identify three moments early in the novella that foreshadow the conclusion. For each: quote the line, name what it predicts, and explain how it works as a literary device.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks how earlier details "prepare the reader for" later events — this is foreshadowing analysis. Tracing structure across a text is a high-value skill.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Copy one sentence that captures the loneliness that runs through this novella. Choose a line that does the work without stating it outright.

💭Prompt 2 · Central Idea & ArgumentSAT: Analysis
Is Steinbeck arguing that the American Dream is impossible for people like George and Lennie — or that it is possible but always fragile? State your claim, then build the argument using at least two pieces of evidence. Address the counterargument.
💡 SAT Skill: Evaluating an author's argument — claim, evidence, counterargument — is a central SAT Reading skill. Practice it here with a real literary text.
🐇Prompt 3 · Diction & ToneSAT: Language
Steinbeck's prose is spare — almost every word does double work. Find a passage of 3–5 sentences where his word choice creates a specific tone (bleak, tender, brutal, ironic). Quote it, name the tone, then analyze three specific words that produce that effect.
💡 SAT Skill: Tone and diction questions appear constantly on the SAT. This exercise trains you to name tone precisely and trace it to specific word choices.

B
An act of mercy and love, protecting Lennie from a crueler fate at the hands of others
C
Evidence that violence is inevitable in a brutal economic system
D

Memoir · Holocaust

🕯️Prompt 1 · Rhetorical Choices in MemoirSAT: Informational Text
Wiesel makes specific rhetorical choices — short sentences, direct address, graphic specificity — to force the reader to witness. Find one passage that exemplifies a deliberate rhetorical choice. Name the device, quote the passage, and explain the intended effect on the reader.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT includes informational/nonfiction texts and asks "why does the author include this detail?" Memoir is nonfiction — the same analytical lens applies.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Choose the single sentence in the memoir that most powerfully conveys Wiesel's central argument. Be prepared to explain exactly why these words — not a summary — are necessary.

🌙Prompt 2 · Title, Symbol & ThemeSAT: Analysis
The word "night" appears throughout the memoir with layered meanings — literal darkness, spiritual crisis, historical horror. Track at least three uses or references to night/darkness. What is Wiesel ultimately saying about what "night" represents in human experience?
💡 SAT Skill: Title analysis and symbol tracking are advanced SAT reading skills. A title is always the author's deliberate first argument about meaning.
✍️Prompt 3 · Synthesis with Historical InformationSAT: Paired Texts
The SAT pairs memoir with historical/informational texts. Find one external fact about the Holocaust — a statistic, policy, or historical event — that deepens a specific passage in Night. Quote the historical fact, quote the Night passage, and explain what each source adds that the other cannot.
💡 SAT Skill: Synthesis across text types is the highest-level SAT Reading skill. What does each source uniquely contribute? What can the memoir do that the historical record cannot?

A

B
Mirror the dehumanization of the camps by stripping language of ornamentation, forcing the reader to confront events directly
C
Make the memoir accessible to younger readers who might struggle with complex vocabulary
D
Reflect Wiesel's belief that emotional language would be inappropriate given the gravity of the subject

Their Eyes Were Watching God

Zora Neale Hurston · 1937 · Choice 3B
Harlem Renaissance
SAT Skills:Dialect & VoiceExtended MetaphorPoint of ViewSynthesis
Status
★★★★★
🌳Prompt 1 · Extended MetaphorSAT: Figurative Language
The novel opens with one of literature's great extended metaphors: "Ships at a distance have every man's wish on board." Analyze this metaphor in full: what is being compared, what does the ship represent for men vs. women, and how does this opening frame the entire novel's argument?
💡 SAT Skill: Extended metaphor analysis is a high-frequency SAT question. You must trace the comparison across multiple sentences — not just identify the device.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Quote the passage where Janie's voice — her own, authentic voice — emerges most powerfully. This should be a moment where you hear her, not the narrator.
Janie's voice quote:
🗣️Prompt 2 · Dialect as Literary DeviceSAT: Language & Craft
Hurston writes dialogue in African American vernacular dialect. Some critics in her time said this was demeaning; Hurston called it authentic and musical. Analyze one dialogue exchange: quote it, then translate it into standard English. What is lost in translation? What does that loss prove about Hurston's choice?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Writing section tests understanding of appropriate tone and word choice for context. This exercise deepens your understanding of how language choices serve purpose.
🔍Prompt 3 · Self-Discovery as Central ThemeSAT: Analysis
Janie's journey is inward as much as outward. At the end of the novel she says she has "been to the horizon and back." What does that mean? Trace her identity across her three marriages — what does she gain or lose at each stage? What does she return with?
💡 SAT Skill: Tracking a character's development across a text mirrors the SAT's "how does the character change between passages" question type.
🎯SAT-Style Question · Their Eyes Were Watching God
The novel's title refers to a moment when Janie and Tea Cake's community watches the coming hurricane. This title most likely functions to suggest that:
A
Religious faith provides the only reliable comfort in times of disaster
B
Characters are simultaneously subject to forces beyond their control and searching for meaning and divine purpose
C
Hurston intends to critique organized religion in Black Southern communities
D
Janie's identity is defined primarily by her religious beliefs rather than her personal relationships

My Choice
SAT Skills:All Reading SkillsSynthesisShort EssayArgument
My Book:
Status

🎯Prompt 1 · Claim + 3 Pieces of EvidenceSAT: Evidence
Write one sentence stating the central idea of your book. Then provide three direct quotes as evidence. For each quote, write one sentence connecting it back to your claim. This is the CEE structure (Claim → Evidence → Explanation) — the foundation of SAT essay writing.
💡 SAT Skill: CEE (Claim-Evidence-Explanation) is the atomic unit of SAT analytical writing. Master this pattern and you can answer any evidence question.
🔗Prompt 2 · Cross-Text ComparisonSAT: Paired Texts
Compare your book to one book from this year's list. Identify a shared theme and a key difference in how each author handles it. Quote both books. Which treatment is more effective and why? This is exactly what SAT paired-passage questions ask.
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-passage synthesis — what do both texts share? how do they differ? — is guaranteed on the SAT Reading section.
🖊️Prompt 3 · SAT Practice Essay (3 Paragraphs)SAT: Essay Writing
Write a focused 3-paragraph analytical response: ¶1 — introduce the book and state your argument. ¶2 — develop with a specific quote and analysis (explain the how, not just the what). ¶3 — explain why this theme matters beyond the page. Target 200–250 words.
💡 SAT Skill: This is a full SAT analytical essay in miniature. Focus on specificity, evidence, and the "so what?" — the significance beyond the story.
