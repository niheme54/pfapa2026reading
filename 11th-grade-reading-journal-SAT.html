The Great Gatsby

F. Scott Fitzgerald · 1925 · Required
American Dream · Symbol
SAT Skills:SymbolismNarrative DistanceArgument AnalysisDiction & Tone
Status
★★★★★
💚Prompt 1 · Symbol TrackingSAT: Reading
The green light, the Valley of Ashes, and the eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg are among literature's most analyzed symbols. Choose two. For each: quote the passage where it appears most powerfully, explain its literal meaning, then its symbolic one, and show how Fitzgerald uses it to develop his central argument about the American Dream.
💡 SAT Skill: Symbol analysis is a high-frequency SAT question. The key move: literal meaning → symbolic meaning → how it serves the author's argument. Never stop at "it represents ___."
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
The SAT requires you to choose the quote that best supports a claim — not just any quote. Select the single sentence that most directly proves Fitzgerald is critiquing, not celebrating, the American Dream.

🎙️Prompt 2 · Narrator Reliability & DistanceSAT: Analysis
Nick Carraway claims to be "one of the few honest people" he has ever known. Is he? Find two moments where Nick's narration seems unreliable or selective. What does he leave out, minimize, or romanticize? What does Fitzgerald gain by choosing this narrator?
💡 SAT Skill: Unreliable narrators test your ability to read between the lines — a core SAT inference skill. The question is always: what does the text reveal that the narrator doesn't intend to reveal?
📐Prompt 3 · Fitzgerald's Argument: Evaluate ItSAT: Argument
The SAT includes argument evaluation questions. Practice here: what is Fitzgerald's central argument about the American Dream? Is it convincing? Identify his strongest piece of evidence (a scene or image) and his weakest point. Does the novel prove its case?
💡 SAT Skill: Evaluating argument quality — claim, strongest evidence, weakest point — is exactly what SAT argument questions test. A strong reader can praise and critique simultaneously.
🎯SAT-Style Question · The Great Gatsby
Nick's final line — "So we beat on, boats against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past" — primarily functions to:
A
Suggest that Nick intends to return to the Midwest and abandon his dreams of success
B
Extend the novel's central metaphor to suggest that all human striving is doomed by our inability to escape the past
C
Imply that Gatsby was right to pursue Daisy despite the tragic outcome
D
Criticize the carelessness of Tom and Daisy specifically for their treatment of Gatsby
📖SAT Vocabulary BuilderSAT: Words in Context
Fitzgerald's prose is dense with precise, elevated vocabulary. For each word: record its part of speech, define it in your own words, and write it in a sentence. The SAT tests words in context — learn them from the page, not flashcards.

The Crucible

Arthur Miller · 1953 · Required
Drama · Allegory · Power
SAT Skills:AllegoryRhetorical PurposeArgumentCharacter Motivation
Status
★★★★★
🔥Prompt 1 · Historical AllegorySAT: Analysis
Miller wrote The Crucible during the McCarthy era as a direct allegory. Identify three specific parallels between the Salem witch trials in the play and McCarthyism in 1950s America. For each: describe the Salem situation, its McCarthyism parallel, and what Miller is arguing about political power and fear.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks you to analyze how an author uses historical or contemporary context to develop meaning. Allegory is the literary version of this — same analytical move.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Find the line where Miller most directly states or implies his argument about how societies manufacture enemies during times of fear. This should be a line a reader could quote to prove Miller's political purpose.
Miller's argument in a single line:
⚖️Prompt 2 · Motivation & Moral CourageSAT: Character Analysis
John Proctor's final decision — to be hanged rather than sign a false confession — is the moral center of the play. What is his reasoning? Write his internal argument as he would make it. Then evaluate: is it a rational choice, an emotional one, or both? Use two lines from the play as evidence.
💡 SAT Skill: Understanding a character's motivation and evaluating the reasoning behind a decision is a direct SAT character-analysis skill. Always ask: what does the character believe, and is that belief logically supported?
📰Prompt 3 · Drama vs. Prose ComparisonSAT: Text Type
The SAT includes both prose and drama excerpts. Identify one technique unique to drama (stage directions, dialogue, acts/scenes) that Miller uses to develop meaning in The Crucible. Quote or describe the moment, name what the technique does, and explain what would be lost if this were a novel instead.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT tests genre awareness — how the form of a text shapes its meaning. Drama's tools (stage directions, physical staging) are different from prose's tools (narration, description).
🎯SAT-Style Question · The Crucible
Miller's stage direction notes in the published script include historical commentary and authorial interpretation. This technique primarily serves to:
A
Compensate for Miller's lack of confidence in his dialogue to convey meaning independently
B
Transform the reading version into a layered historical argument, allowing Miller to explicitly guide the reader's interpretation of events
C
Demonstrate Miller's academic background as a historian of the colonial period
D
Provide theatrical cues for directors that would otherwise be impossible to infer from the dialogue alone

Invisible Man

Ralph Ellison · 1952 · Choice 3A
Identity · Race · America
SAT Skills:First-Person NarrationSymbolismSatireComplex Inference
Status
★★★★★
👁️Prompt 1 · Prologue AnalysisSAT: Close Reading
The prologue begins: "I am an invisible man." Ellison immediately complicates this — the narrator is not literally invisible. Analyze the first three paragraphs of the prologue as if it were a SAT passage: What is the narrator's central claim? What evidence does he offer? What effect does Ellison create by opening this way?
💡 SAT Skill: Opening paragraph analysis is a guaranteed SAT question. The author always makes deliberate choices about how to begin — your job is to explain why those choices matter.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Select the quote that best captures what "invisibility" really means in this novel — not physical, but social and psychological. This quote should be one you could use to prove Ellison's argument about race and recognition in America.
Defining the invisibility quote:
🎭Prompt 2 · Satire & IronySAT: Figurative Language
Ellison uses biting satire throughout — the Battle Royal, the Brotherhood, the college. Choose one satirical scene. Describe what it literally depicts, what institution or idea it is satirizing, and how Ellison uses irony to make his critique more powerful than a direct argument would be.
💡 SAT Skill: Irony and satire questions appear on the SAT. Key move: what does the text say vs. what does it mean? The gap between the two is where the meaning lives.
🔦Prompt 3 · The Ending: Ambiguity as ArgumentSAT: Interpretation
The narrator retreats underground at the end, saying he plans to emerge. Is this resolution hopeful, despairing, or deliberately unresolved? Write two interpretations of the ending — one that reads it as hopeful, one as tragic. Then argue which Ellison intends and why, citing evidence.
💡 SAT Skill: Ambiguous endings are a staple of SAT literary passages. You must hold two possible interpretations simultaneously and then argue for one — exactly what this prompt asks.

B
Suggest that American society has denied him a recognized identity, making him a representative figure rather than an individual
C
Indicate that the narrator has not yet decided what kind of person he wants to become
D
Allow Ellison to draw from his own biography without making the connection explicit

🔴Prompt 1 · Atwood's Claim: Nothing NewSAT: Argument
Atwood has said that every event in The Handmaid's Tale has a historical precedent — she invented nothing. Choose three specific elements of Gilead and research or identify their real-world parallels. How does knowing this affect your reading of the novel as argument rather than fantasy?
💡 SAT Skill: Evaluating an author's central claim and the evidence they use to support it is core SAT work. Here, the claim is embedded in the world-building itself.

📜Prompt 2 · The Historical Notes as FrameSAT: Structure
The novel ends with "Historical Notes" — a future academic's analysis of Offred's tapes. How does this frame change the meaning of everything before it? What does it reveal about how history is recorded, who records it, and what gets lost? How does structure itself become an argument here?
💡 SAT Skill: Frame structure questions — "what does the author achieve by beginning/ending this way?" — are a high-value SAT question type. The structure is always part of the argument.
🧵Prompt 3 · Language as ControlSAT: Language
Gilead controls language — names, titles, forbidden words, approved phrases. Choose two examples of Atwood's language control (Handmaid names, Salvaging, "Blessed be the fruit," etc.). For each: what does the real meaning of the language do to the speaker and listener? What is Atwood arguing about the relationship between language and power?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Writing section tests your understanding that word choice is a power decision. Atwood makes this argument visible — studying her deepens your language instincts.
🎯SAT-Style Question · The Handmaid's Tale

A

B
Acknowledge the constructed nature of all testimony and memory while reinforcing the authenticity of lived experience under oppression
C

D
Demonstrate that women in Gilead have been so thoroughly conditioned that they can no longer accurately perceive their own experiences

The Scarlet Letter

Nathaniel Hawthorne · 1850 · Choice C
Puritanism · Sin · Symbolism
SAT Skills:Extended SymbolAllegoryNarrator's StanceDiction & Tone
Status
★★★★★
🔴Prompt 1 · The Letter as Extended SymbolSAT: Symbolism
The scarlet "A" begins as a mark of shame but accumulates new meanings as the novel progresses — Able, Angel, Adultery, and more. Trace the letter's symbolic evolution across at least three moments in the novel. For each: what does the letter mean at that point, who assigns that meaning, and how does this shift reflect Hawthorne's argument about the difference between public judgment and private truth?
💡 SAT Skill: Extended symbol analysis — tracking how a single image accumulates meaning across a text — is one of the SAT's most sophisticated literary questions. The "A" is the perfect training case because its evolution is deliberate and traceable.
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
⛪Prompt 2 · The Crucible & The Scarlet Letter: Puritan HypocrisySAT: Paired Texts
Both The Scarlet Letter and The Crucible are set in Puritan New England and both expose the gap between public religious morality and private human reality. Compare how Hawthorne and Miller each dramatize this hypocrisy. Quote one passage from each. Which portrayal is more damning of Puritan society, and why?
💡 SAT Skill: This is a natural SAT paired-passage setup — two authors, one historical moment, different literary forms (novel vs. drama), same target. The comparison trains the exact analytical move the SAT rewards.
🌲Prompt 3 · Nature vs. SocietySAT: Setting
Hawthorne consistently contrasts the forest and the town. The town represents law, judgment, and repression; the forest represents freedom, truth, and danger. Find two scenes — one in each setting — and analyze how Hawthorne uses physical space to carry moral meaning. What does this nature/society contrast argue about where human beings are most truly themselves?
💡 SAT Skill: Setting as symbolic space — how a physical environment carries moral or psychological meaning — is a recurring SAT literary analysis concept. Hawthorne uses it more deliberately than almost any other American novelist.

Between the World and Me

Ta-Nehisi Coates · 2015 · Choice D
Essay · Memoir · Letter Form
SAT Skills:Rhetorical AnalysisLetter FormAuthor's PurposeArgument
Status
★★★★★
✉️Prompt 1 · The Letter Form as ArgumentSAT: Structure
Coates writes as a letter to his teenage son Samori. Why a letter? What does addressing a specific person — rather than writing an essay to the general public — allow Coates to do that a traditional argument format wouldn't? Find two moments where the intimacy of the letter form carries the argument more powerfully than statistics or abstract claims could.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks how an author's structural choice — form, audience, point of view — shapes the argument's effectiveness. The letter is a form choice, and Coates's choice is deliberate and analyzable.
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
🔗Prompt 2 · Coates & The Great Gatsby: The American DreamSAT: Synthesis
Fitzgerald exposes the American Dream as a beautiful lie in 1925; Coates dissects what that lie has always meant for Black Americans in 2015. Compare how each author attacks the American Dream myth. Quote one passage from each. What does reading them 90 years apart reveal about how the promise of America functions differently depending on who is doing the dreaming?
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-passage synthesis across a long time gap is a classic SAT move — the contrast in historical moment is part of the argument. What stays the same across 90 years is as significant as what changes.
💪Prompt 3 · The Body as Central MetaphorSAT: Extended Metaphor
Coates returns again and again to the physical body — its vulnerability, its beauty, its meaning under threat. Identify three moments where the body is central to his argument. For each: what is literally happening, and what larger claim does Coates make through this physical reality? What does grounding his argument in the body accomplish that abstract language about "rights" or "justice" couldn't?
💡 SAT Skill: Extended metaphor — a single image or concept that recurs and deepens across a text — is tested on the SAT. Coates's "body" is a masterclass in how to build an argument through a sustained, concrete metaphor.

Beloved

Toni Morrison · 1987 · Choice E
Memory · Trauma · Freedom
SAT Skills:Non-Linear NarrativeComplex InferenceImageryAmbiguity
Status
★★★★★
👻Prompt 1 · Memory as HauntingSAT: Analysis
Morrison opens with "124 was spiteful." The house is haunted — literally and metaphorically. Analyze how Morrison uses the supernatural as a way of representing trauma and repressed memory. Find two moments where the ghost/Beloved functions as more than a plot device. What is Morrison arguing about what happens to people — and communities — when they cannot process their trauma?
💡 SAT Skill: When a text uses the supernatural, the SAT always asks what it represents beyond its literal function. Beloved is the definitive example of supernatural-as-psychological-argument.
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
🌊Prompt 2 · Non-Linear Time & the SAT ReaderSAT: Structure
Morrison refuses to tell the story in chronological order — the past keeps erupting into the present. Identify three moments where the narrative jumps in time. For each: what do we learn from that flashback or memory, and what would be lost if Morrison had told it in sequence? What is she arguing about how trauma actually works in the human mind?
💡 SAT Skill: Structure questions — why does the author organize the narrative this way? — appear on every SAT. Non-linear structure is always a deliberate argument about how time, memory, or consciousness works.
🔗Prompt 3 · Morrison, Coates & Ellison: The Black American ExperienceSAT: Synthesis
Toni Morrison (1987), Ta-Nehisi Coates (2015), and Ralph Ellison (1952) each write about the experience of being Black in America, but through radically different forms — novel, essay-letter, and satirical fiction. Compare one specific theme across any two of these three works. Quote both. What does the form each author chose tell you about what they believed literature could accomplish?
💡 SAT Skill: This is the 11th grade capstone synthesis: comparing how two authors handle the same theme through different literary forms. Form is always a choice — and a choice is always an argument.

🎯Prompt 1 · Central Claim + 3-Point EvidenceSAT: Evidence
State your book's central argument in one sentence. Then build three-point support: for each piece of evidence, copy the exact quote, then write one sentence explaining the connection to your claim. This is CEE — the core SAT analytical writing structure.
💡 SAT Skill: Claim → Evidence → Explanation (CEE) is the single most important writing pattern for the SAT. Mastering it here means you can apply it anywhere.
🔗Prompt 2 · Synthesis: Your Book + American LiteratureSAT: Synthesis
Synthesize your independent book with one of the required texts. What shared theme connects them? Find a quote from each that speaks to that theme — then identify the most important difference in how each author handles it. Which author's approach is more persuasive, and why?
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-passage synthesis — comparing how two authors develop a shared idea — is the highest-difficulty SAT Reading question. This prompt is direct preparation.
🖊️Prompt 3 · Full SAT Practice Essay (3 Paragraphs)SAT: Essay
Write a complete 3-paragraph analytical essay about your book. ¶1: Introduce the book, state a specific analytical thesis (not just a topic). ¶2: Develop your thesis with a direct quote and a full CEE explanation. ¶3: Address significance — what does this theme reveal about the human experience beyond this specific book? Target: 250–300 words. Precision over length.
💡 SAT Skill: This is a full SAT-style analytical essay. The key: specificity in your thesis, direct evidence, and a "so what" conclusion that shows you understand the text's larger significance.
