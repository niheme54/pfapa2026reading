Hamlet

William Shakespeare · c. 1600 · Required
Drama · Philosophy · Grief
SAT Skills:Soliloquy AnalysisDramatic IronyArgument StructureLanguage Precision
Status
★★★★★
💀Prompt 1 · Soliloquy as ArgumentSAT: Reading
"To be, or not to be" is not a question about suicide — it is a philosophical argument about the cost of consciousness. Break it down as you would a SAT argument passage: What is Hamlet's central claim? What evidence does he offer (his examples of suffering)? What is his conclusion, and does the logic hold?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT includes philosophical and argumentative passages. A soliloquy is an argument made visible — dissecting it trains the exact same analytical move: claim → evidence → conclusion → evaluate.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Find the single line from Hamlet that best encapsulates the play's central philosophical tension — not about the plot, but about what it means to be a conscious, moral being. A line that could stand alone as an insight about human experience.

🎭Prompt 2 · Dramatic Irony & the Reader's AdvantageSAT: Analysis
Dramatic irony occurs when the audience knows something the characters do not. Identify three moments in Hamlet where dramatic irony operates. For each: what do we know that the character doesn't, and how does Shakespeare use that gap to create meaning or tension?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT frequently tests dramatic irony — understanding the gap between what characters know and what the reader knows. This is an inference skill: what does the text reveal that isn't explicitly stated?
🔬Prompt 3 · Shakespeare's Language: Unpacking a SpeechSAT: Language
Choose any 8–10 lines from the play. Translate them into plain modern English (literal meaning only). Then identify three moments where the poetic language — the metaphor, the rhythm, the word choice — carries meaning that your translation loses. What does this prove about the relationship between form and content?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT tests your ability to identify how an author's specific word choices carry meaning beyond their literal definitions. Shakespeare is the ultimate training ground for this skill.
🎯SAT-Style Question · Hamlet
Hamlet's repeated delays in avenging his father's murder have been interpreted in many ways. Which interpretation is most directly supported by the text of Hamlet's soliloquies?
A
Hamlet is a physical coward who fears death above all else
B
Hamlet is paralyzed by an excess of moral and philosophical consciousness — he thinks too precisely on the event
C
Hamlet doubts the ghost's account and requires absolute certainty before acting
D
Hamlet secretly loves Claudius and cannot bring himself to harm a father figure
📖SAT Vocabulary BuilderSAT: Words in Context
Shakespeare's vocabulary is one of the richest in literature — and many of his words appear on the SAT. Record word, part of speech, context definition, and an original sentence. Learn the word as it operates in context, not in isolation.

Crime and Punishment

Fyodor Dostoevsky · 1866 · Required
Psychology · Guilt · Redemption
SAT Skills:Complex InferenceInterior MonologueArgument EvaluationPsychological Analysis
Status
★★★★★
🧠Prompt 1 · Raskolnikov's Theory: Evaluate the ArgumentSAT: Argument
Raskolnikov believes "extraordinary" people have the right to transgress moral law for a greater good. Reconstruct his argument formally: What is his central claim? What assumptions does it rest on? What evidence does he cite? Then identify the logical flaw that Dostoevsky exposes through the novel's events.
💡 SAT Skill: Identifying a flawed argument's logic — unstated assumptions, weak evidence, faulty reasoning — is a core SAT skill. Dostoevsky builds an entire novel around dismantling one flawed argument.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Find the passage where Raskolnikov's psychological deterioration is most visible in his language — where the prose itself breaks down or fragments. This is where Dostoevsky's interior technique is most powerful.

🔄Prompt 2 · Sonya as CounterargumentSAT: Analysis
Sonya represents Dostoevsky's answer to Raskolnikov's theory. What is her implicit argument about moral law, suffering, and redemption? She never gives a philosophical lecture — her argument is embodied in her actions and life. Reconstruct it: what does she believe? How does her existence refute his theory?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT sometimes asks how one character or perspective functions as a counterargument to another. This requires understanding both positions with equal precision.
🌆Prompt 3 · St. Petersburg as CharacterSAT: Setting & Atmosphere
Dostoevsky's St. Petersburg is not backdrop — it is an active force. Quote two descriptions of the city. For each: what does the description say literally, what does it suggest about the psychological state of its inhabitants, and how does setting function as an extension of Raskolnikov's mind?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks how setting contributes to atmosphere, character psychology, and theme. Setting-as-mirror is a sophisticated analytical concept this prompt develops directly.
🎯SAT-Style Question · Crime and Punishment
Raskolnikov confesses to Sonya before he confesses to the police. This sequence of confession most likely serves to suggest that:
A
Raskolnikov is primarily motivated by a fear of legal punishment rather than moral guilt
B
Authentic redemption requires moral and spiritual acknowledgment before legal accountability — confession to a witness of conscience precedes confession to society
C
Dostoevsky believed the Russian Orthodox Church held greater authority than the state
D
Raskolnikov loves Sonya and wants her to know the truth before she hears it from others

Things Fall Apart

Chinua Achebe · 1958 · Choice 3A
Postcolonial · Nigeria · Identity
SAT Skills:Paired TextsPostcolonial ArgumentCultural ContextNarrator Perspective
Status
★★★★★
🌍Prompt 1 · Achebe vs. Conrad: Paired ArgumentSAT: Paired Texts
Achebe wrote Things Fall Apart explicitly to answer Conrad's Heart of Darkness — to tell Africa's story from the inside. Without reading Heart of Darkness, you can still engage this: Find two passages in Things Fall Apart where Achebe gives Igbo culture complexity, ritual, and humanity that a colonial narrative would erase. What is Achebe arguing by showing us this?
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-text synthesis — understanding how one text responds to or argues against another — is the highest-difficulty SAT reading skill. This prompt builds exactly that.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Find the passage that most powerfully demonstrates what is lost when the colonial world displaces Okonkwo's world. This should be a moment that makes the reader grieve what colonialism destroys — not just politically, but humanly.

⚡Prompt 2 · Okonkwo's Tragic FlawSAT: Character Analysis
Okonkwo is a tragic hero in the classical sense — his greatest strength is also his fatal flaw. Define his central value (what does he believe masculinity, strength, and success require?). Then show how that value, while understandable, systematically destroys everything he loves. Quote the moment that best illustrates this irony.
💡 SAT Skill: Tragic irony — when a character's strength becomes their undoing — is a sophisticated analytical concept. The SAT expects you to track character values and trace their consequences precisely.
📖Prompt 3 · The Title's ArgumentSAT: Language
Achebe's title comes from Yeats's poem "The Second Coming": "Things fall apart; the centre cannot hold." Research this poem's context (written after WWI). How does placing an Irish post-war poem at the center of a Nigerian colonial novel create meaning? What is Achebe saying about the relationship between the violence Europe inflicted on itself and the violence it inflicted on Africa?
💡 SAT Skill: Allusion analysis — understanding how a reference to another text creates layered meaning — is an advanced SAT reading skill. The title is always the author's first argument.
🎯SAT-Style Question · Things Fall Apart
The District Commissioner's plan to include a paragraph about Okonkwo in his book about African primitivism at the novel's end most likely serves to:
A
Show that Okonkwo's story will eventually be recognized and honored by Western scholars
B
Illustrate how colonialism reduces a rich, complex human life to a data point in a European narrative — the ultimate erasure Achebe's novel resists
C
Suggest that the District Commissioner genuinely respects Igbo culture despite his role in destroying it
D
Indicate that written records, even colonial ones, are better than no record at all

One Hundred Years of Solitude

Gabriel García Márquez · 1967 · Choice 3B
Magical Realism · Latin America
SAT Skills:Magical RealismNarrative TimeAmbiguityComplex Inference
Status
★★★★★
✨Prompt 1 · Magical Realism as ArgumentSAT: Analysis
In magical realism, supernatural events are narrated in the same deadpan tone as ordinary ones. Choose two magical events (yellow butterflies, the levitating priest, blood tracing its way home, the insomnia plague). For each: what is literally happening? What does it represent in the real world of Latin American history or human psychology? What does the magic allow García Márquez to say that realism couldn't?
💡 SAT Skill: When SAT passages use unusual narrative techniques, you must ask: what does this technique achieve that a different technique wouldn't? This is the core analytical question about form and function.
📌Cite the TextSAT: Evidence
Find the opening sentence of the novel: "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice." Unpack why this one sentence is considered one of the greatest opening lines in world literature. What does it do in a single sentence?

🔁Prompt 2 · Cyclical Time & RepetitionSAT: Structure
The Buendía family repeats the same names, same obsessions, same mistakes across seven generations. What is García Márquez arguing about history and human nature through this structural choice? Is the repetition pessimistic (we are doomed to repeat) or something more complex? Use two specific examples of repetition from the novel.
💡 SAT Skill: Structure-as-argument is an advanced SAT concept. When an author repeats patterns across a text, the repetition itself carries meaning — you must interpret it, not just notice it.
📜Prompt 3 · Solitude as ThemeSAT: Theme Analysis
Every Buendía is fundamentally alone — even in love, even in crowds. Identify three different characters and describe how solitude manifests differently for each. Then synthesize: what is García Márquez's ultimate statement about the human condition and solitude? Is this a novel of despair, of acceptance, or of something harder to name?
💡 SAT Skill: Synthesizing a theme across multiple characters and events — rather than from a single passage — is the most sophisticated SAT reading task. This prompt builds exactly that capacity.
🎯SAT-Style Question · One Hundred Years of Solitude
The novel ends with Aureliano deciphering Melquíades's manuscripts and discovering that every event of the family's history has already been written. This ending most likely serves to suggest:
A
That Melquíades is the true protagonist of the novel and its events are his invention
B
That history is written and predetermined — and that recognizing this pattern is itself the final moment of consciousness before erasure
C
That García Márquez believes all human choices are ultimately meaningless
D
That the Buendía family's destruction is a punishment for their collective moral failures

🎯Prompt 1 · The Author's Central ArgumentSAT: Argument
What is your author's central argument about human existence? State it in one sentence. Then build the case as the author builds it: identify the three most important pieces of evidence (scenes, images, passages) and explain precisely how each one develops the argument. This is full CEE at the senior level.
💡 SAT Skill: At the senior level, the SAT expects you to identify not just theme but authorial argument — the specific claim about human experience the text is making. This is the highest analytical register.
🌐Prompt 2 · World Literature SynthesisSAT: Synthesis
This is your senior synthesis exercise. Connect your independent book to two books from this year's list — one thematic connection, one contrast. Quote all three books. Then answer: what does reading these books together reveal that none of them could reveal alone?
💡 SAT Skill: Multi-text synthesis — finding what three sources together reveal — is the most advanced SAT reading skill and the foundation of college-level literary study. This is where you're headed.
🖊️Prompt 3 · Senior Analytical Essay (4 Paragraphs)SAT: College-Level Essay
Write a four-paragraph analytical essay. ¶1: Introduce the book, its context, and your specific analytical thesis (an arguable claim, not just a topic). ¶2: First supporting point — direct quote + full analysis (explain the how and the why, not just the what). ¶3: Second supporting point — different technique or angle, direct quote + analysis. ¶4: Conclusion that explains the book's significance beyond itself — why does this argument matter for how we understand the human experience? Target: 320–400 words. This is your college essay warm-up.
💡 SAT Skill: This is a full SAT-level analytical essay — the kind that earns top scores. Specificity, direct evidence, and a "so what" conclusion that transcends the text are what distinguish a strong essay from an adequate one.
