Summer
Reading

Curated reading lists for each high school grade — books chosen to challenge, inspire, and stay with you long after the last page.

09

Freshman Year

Big ideas, big worlds. These books open the door to literary analysis, moral complexity, and stories that refuse easy answers.

01 / Required
To Kill a Mockingbird
Harper Lee
Scout Finch's Alabama childhood becomes a lens on justice, race, and moral courage. A masterclass in point of view.
Classic · Justice
02 / Required
The Outsiders
S.E. Hinton
Class, loyalty, and identity collide in this raw, urgent novel written by a 16-year-old. Deceptively simple, deeply true.
Coming of Age
03 / Choose One
The House on Mango Street
Sandra Cisneros
Vignettes of a young Latina girl's world in Chicago. Each chapter is a gem; together they form a mosaic of belonging and longing.
Vignette · Identity
03 / Choose One
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry
Mildred D. Taylor
The Logan family fights to keep their land and dignity in Depression-era Mississippi. Gripping, historically rich, emotionally lasting.
Historical Fiction
04 / Independent
I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings
Maya Angelou
A memoir of extraordinary resilience. Angelou's voice is unlike any other — lyrical, brave, and utterly unforgettable.
Memoir · Voice
05 / Independent
Ender's Game
Orson Scott Card
A gifted child is trained to fight an alien war — but nothing is as it seems. Raises deep questions about leadership and manipulation.
Sci-Fi · Ethics

Tips for 9th Graders

  • Keep a reading journal — jot one surprising idea per chapter
  • Notice how authors use point of view to control what you know
  • Look up unfamiliar historical context; it deepens everything
  • Don't rush — savor the language, not just the plot
10

Sophomore Year

Sharpen your analytical eye. These books reward close reading — symbols, structure, and subtext are waiting on every page.

01 / Required
Lord of the Flies
William Golding
Stranded boys reveal civilization's thin skin. Rich in allegory and symbolism — every element carries double meaning.
Allegory · Human Nature
02 / Required
Of Mice and Men
John Steinbeck
A slim, devastating novella about dreams, loneliness, and the mercy of friendship. Every word earns its place.
Novella · American Dream
03 / Choose One
Night
Elie Wiesel
A Holocaust survivor's witness testimony, stripped to its essence. Unsparing and essential — read slowly, with care.
Memoir · Holocaust
03 / Choose One
Their Eyes Were Watching God
Zora Neale Hurston
Janie Crawford searches for herself across marriages and storms. Hurston's dialogue in dialect is a revelation — read it aloud.
Harlem Renaissance
04 / Independent
Animal Farm
George Orwell
The fable is deceptively cheerful; the political satire is razor-sharp. A perfect introduction to allegory and power.
Satire · Power
05 / Independent
The Kite Runner
Khaled Hosseini
Guilt, redemption, and Afghanistan's transformation. One of the most emotionally complex first-person narratives in modern fiction.
Redemption · History

Tips for 10th Graders

  • Annotate as you read — underline images and patterns
  • Ask: what is this book actually about beneath the surface?
  • Compare authors' styles — how does Golding differ from Steinbeck?
  • Try writing one paragraph of analysis per book
11

Junior Year

American literature in focus — and works that speak to power, identity, and the world you're inheriting. College-level reading begins here.

01 / Required
The Great Gatsby
F. Scott Fitzgerald
The American Dream dissected through lavish parties and a green light across the bay. Every sentence is doing three things at once.
American Dream · Symbol
02 / Required
The Crucible
Arthur Miller
Salem's witch trials as McCarthyism allegory. A drama about fear, conformity, and what we sacrifice to protect our name.
Drama · Allegory
03 / Choose One
Invisible Man
Ralph Ellison
A nameless Black man navigates a society that refuses to see him. One of the great American novels — ambitious, surreal, essential.
Identity · Race
03 / Choose One
The Handmaid's Tale
Margaret Atwood
A dystopian society that controls women's bodies and voices. Atwood's worldbuilding is meticulous and disturbingly plausible.
Dystopia · Gender
04 / Independent
Between the World and Me
Ta-Nehisi Coates
A letter from father to son about being Black in America. Coates writes with ferocity and precision — not an easy read, a necessary one.
Nonfiction · Race
05 / Independent
Beloved
Toni Morrison
Slavery's ghost haunts a freed woman in post-Civil War Ohio. Morrison's prose is demanding — and among the most beautiful ever written.
Literary Fiction · Trauma

Tips for 11th Graders

  • Practice thesis-driven analysis — one claim, three pieces of evidence
  • Read the historical context before you begin each book
  • Start a vocabulary list — SAT words show up in these texts
  • Consider how each author uses structure, not just story
12

Senior Year

World literature, philosophical weight, and books that ask the hardest questions. Read like the thinker you're becoming.

01 / Required
Hamlet
William Shakespeare
The original introspective hero — grief, revenge, inaction, and mortality in five acts. Read it aloud with someone. It transforms.
Drama · Philosophy
02 / Required
Crime and Punishment
Fyodor Dostoevsky
A murderer's psychological unraveling across 550 pages. Dense but riveting — the portrait of a mind at war with itself.
Russian Lit · Psychology
03 / Choose One
Things Fall Apart
Chinua Achebe
Colonialism through Igbo eyes. Achebe writes against the Conrad tradition — this is Africa telling its own story. Precise and powerful.
Postcolonial · Nigeria
03 / Choose One
One Hundred Years of Solitude
Gabriel García Márquez
Seven generations of the Buendía family in Macondo. Magical realism at its most intoxicating — don't fight the rules of this world, enter them.
Magical Realism
04 / Independent
The Brothers Karamazov
Fyodor Dostoevsky
God, free will, suffering, and family — the big questions in one immense novel. The "Grand Inquisitor" chapter alone justifies the read.
Philosophy · Faith
05 / Independent
The Road
Cormac McCarthy
A father and son walk through a burned America carrying "the fire." McCarthy's stripped prose and its moral core will stay with you forever.
Dystopia · Parenthood

Tips for 12th Graders

  • Connect themes across books — what patterns do you notice?
  • Think about how these books changed you, not just what they said
  • Write your college essays alongside your reading — they feed each other
  • Discuss with someone — these books are meant to be argued about
