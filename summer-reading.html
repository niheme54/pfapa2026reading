Summer
Reading

Curated reading lists for each high school grade — 8 books per grade, including Shakespeare at every level, contemporary voices, American classics, and world literature. All chosen to challenge, inspire, and stay with you.

09

Freshman Year

Big ideas, big worlds. These books open the door to literary analysis, moral complexity, and stories that refuse easy answers.

01 / Required
To Kill a Mockingbird
Harper Lee
Scout Finch's Alabama childhood becomes a lens on justice, race, and moral courage. A masterclass in point of view and unreliable narration.
Classic · Justice
02 / Required
The Outsiders
S.E. Hinton
Class, loyalty, and identity collide in this raw, urgent novel written by a 16-year-old. Deceptively simple, deeply true.
Coming of Age
03A / Choose One
The House on Mango Street
Sandra Cisneros
Vignettes of a young Latina girl's world in Chicago. Each chapter is a gem; together they form a mosaic of belonging and longing.
Vignette · Identity
03B / Choose One
Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry
Mildred D. Taylor
The Logan family fights to keep their land and dignity in Depression-era Mississippi. Gripping, historically rich, emotionally lasting.
Historical Fiction
03C / Choose One
Romeo and Juliet
William Shakespeare
The original star-crossed lovers — and a masterclass in poetic language, fate vs. free will, and the power of family loyalty. Read it aloud for full effect.
Shakespeare · Drama
03D / Choose One
The Watsons Go to Birmingham — 1963
Christopher Paul Curtis
The Watson family drives from Flint, Michigan to Birmingham, Alabama — and arrives just in time for the civil rights movement's darkest moment. Funny, warm, and devastating all at once.
Civil Rights · Family
03E / Choose One
Joey Pigza Swallowed the Key
Jack Gantos
Joey has ADHD and a heart the size of a house. Gantos writes from inside Joey's mind — fast, funny, honest, and unexpectedly moving.
ADHD · Empathy

Your Choice
Student selection

Independent · Open

Tips for 9th Graders

  • Keep a reading journal — jot one surprising idea per chapter
  • Notice how authors use point of view to shape what you know
  • For Romeo & Juliet: read it aloud — the language unlocks when you hear it
  • For The Watsons Go to Birmingham: notice how the tone shifts — Curtis uses humor deliberately before the novel's heartbreak arrives
  • Look up unfamiliar historical context; it deepens everything
  • Don't rush — savor the language, not just the plot
10

Sophomore Year

Sharpen your analytical eye. These books reward close reading — symbols, structure, and subtext are waiting on every page.

01 / Required
Lord of the Flies
William Golding
Stranded boys reveal civilization's thin skin. Rich in allegory and symbolism — every element carries double meaning.
Allegory · Human Nature
02 / Required
Of Mice and Men
John Steinbeck
A slim, devastating novella about dreams, loneliness, and the mercy of friendship. Every word earns its place.
Novella · American Dream
03A / Choose One
Night
Elie Wiesel
A Holocaust survivor's witness testimony, stripped to its essence. Unsparing and essential — read slowly, with care.
Memoir · Holocaust
03B / Choose One
Their Eyes Were Watching God
Zora Neale Hurston
Janie Crawford searches for herself across marriages and storms. Hurston's dialogue in dialect is a revelation — read it aloud.
Harlem Renaissance
03C / Choose One
Julius Caesar
William Shakespeare
Betrayal, rhetoric, and the price of ambition. Antony's funeral speech is one of the most analyzed pieces of persuasion in all of literature — and for good reason.
Shakespeare · Rhetoric
03D / Choose One
The Autobiography of Malcolm X
Malcolm X & Alex Haley
One of the most powerful first-person accounts in American history. Malcolm X transforms across this book — in identity, belief, and vision — and takes you with him.
Memoir · Rhetoric
03E / Choose One
Uncle Tom's Cabin
Harriet Beecher Stowe
The novel Abraham Lincoln credited with starting the Civil War. Read it as political argument as much as fiction — then read it critically for its era's limitations.
Abolitionist · Historical
04 / Independent
Your Choice
Student selection
Animal Farm, The Kite Runner, or any teacher-approved title. Record your choice in the journal and complete the independent reading prompts.
Independent · Open

Tips for 10th Graders

  • Annotate as you read — underline images and patterns
  • Ask: what is this book actually about beneath the surface?
  • For Julius Caesar: listen to Antony's speech and map his rhetorical moves
  • For Malcolm X: notice how his prose style shifts as his worldview changes
  • Compare authors' styles — how does Golding differ from Steinbeck?
  • Try writing one paragraph of analysis per book
11

Junior Year

American literature in focus — and works that speak to power, identity, and the world you're inheriting. College-level reading begins here.

01 / Required
The Great Gatsby
F. Scott Fitzgerald
The American Dream dissected through lavish parties and a green light across the bay. Every sentence is doing three things at once.
American Dream · Symbol
02 / Required
The Crucible
Arthur Miller
Salem's witch trials as McCarthyism allegory. A drama about fear, conformity, and what we sacrifice to protect our name.
Drama · Allegory
03A / Choose One
Invisible Man
Ralph Ellison
A nameless Black man navigates a society that refuses to see him. One of the great American novels — ambitious, surreal, essential.
Identity · Race
03B / Choose One
The Handmaid's Tale
Margaret Atwood
A dystopian society that controls women's bodies and voices. Atwood's worldbuilding is meticulous and disturbingly plausible.
Dystopia · Gender
03C / Choose One
The Scarlet Letter
Nathaniel Hawthorne
Hester Prynne bears public shame in Puritan Boston while her accusers hide their own sins. A devastating meditation on hypocrisy, guilt, and the difference between public and private truth.
American Classic · Symbol
03D / Choose One
Between the World and Me
Ta-Nehisi Coates
A letter from father to son about being Black in America. Coates writes with ferocity and precision — not an easy read, a necessary one.
Essay · Race · Letter Form
03E / Choose One
Beloved
Toni Morrison
Slavery's ghost haunts a freed woman in post-Civil War Ohio. Morrison's prose is demanding — and among the most beautiful ever written.
Literary Fiction · Memory
04 / Independent
Your Choice
Student selection
Any teacher-approved title. The independent reading section of your journal includes a full 3-paragraph SAT essay prompt — choose a book that gives you something to argue about.
Independent · Open

Tips for 11th Graders

  • Practice thesis-driven analysis — one claim, three pieces of evidence
  • Read The Scarlet Letter alongside The Crucible — both are set in Puritan New England and they talk to each other across 300 years
  • For Coates: read it slowly. Every paragraph is an argument, not just a story
  • For Beloved: don't fight the non-linear structure — Morrison is making an argument about how memory works
  • Start a vocabulary list — SAT words show up in every one of these texts
12

Senior Year

World literature, philosophical weight, and books that ask the hardest questions. Read like the thinker you're becoming.

01 / Required
Hamlet
William Shakespeare
The original introspective hero — grief, revenge, inaction, and mortality in five acts. Read it aloud with someone. It transforms.
Drama · Philosophy
02 / Required
Crime and Punishment
Fyodor Dostoevsky
A murderer's psychological unraveling across 550 pages. Dense but riveting — the portrait of a mind at war with itself.
Russian Lit · Psychology
03A / Choose One
Things Fall Apart
Chinua Achebe
Colonialism through Igbo eyes. Achebe writes against the Conrad tradition — this is Africa telling its own story. Precise and powerful.
Postcolonial · Nigeria
03B / Choose One
One Hundred Years of Solitude
Gabriel García Márquez
Seven generations of the Buendía family in Macondo. Magical realism at its most intoxicating — don't fight the rules of this world, enter them.
Magical Realism
03C / Choose One
Macbeth
William Shakespeare
Ambition, guilt, and the blood that won't wash off. Pairs powerfully with Hamlet — two Shakespeare tragedies, opposite failure modes, the same devastating end.
Shakespeare · Tragedy
03D / Choose One
The Brothers Karamazov
Fyodor Dostoevsky
God, free will, suffering, and family — the big questions in one immense novel. The "Grand Inquisitor" chapter alone justifies the read. Pairs with Crime & Punishment.
Philosophy · Faith
03E / Choose One
The Road
Cormac McCarthy
A father and son walk through a burned America carrying "the fire." McCarthy's prose is stripped to bone — every word doing maximum work. Unforgettable.
Post-Apocalyptic · Survival
04 / Independent
Your Choice
Student selection
Any teacher-approved title. The 12th grade independent journal includes a full 4-paragraph college-prep analytical essay — choose a book worth arguing about.
Independent · Open

Tips for 12th Graders

  • Connect themes across books — Hamlet and Macbeth, Crime and Punishment and Brothers Karamazov: the patterns are intentional
  • For The Road: don't look for plot. Look for what McCarthy is arguing about what makes life worth living
  • Think about how these books changed you, not just what they said
  • Write your college essays alongside your reading — these books will give you language for your own life
  • Discuss with someone — every one of these books is designed to be argued about
