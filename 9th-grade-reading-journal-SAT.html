Classic · Justice

🧭Prompt 1 · Point of ViewSAT: Reading
Scout sees events through a child's eyes. Find one scene where her limited understanding reveals something adults in the scene miss entirely. Quote the passage, then explain what it shows.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks you to identify how a narrator's perspective shapes meaning. Practice citing the specific line — not just summarizing — to build evidence skills.

⚖️Prompt 2 · Theme & Author's PurposeSAT: Analysis
Atticus says courage is knowing you're beaten before you start but fighting anyway. How does Lee use Atticus to develop the theme of moral courage? What does the author want the reader to take away?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT tests "author's purpose" — why did the author include this? Practice distinguishing what happens (plot) from what it means (theme/purpose).
🌿Prompt 3 · Figurative Language & SymbolsSAT: Language
Identify the mockingbird as a symbol. Then find one example of simile, metaphor, or personification in the novel. Write the exact quote, name the device, and explain the effect it creates for the reader.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Reading section asks what a word, phrase, or image contributes to the text. The Writing section tests your own use of these devices.

It makes the story unreliable because the narrator's memory is imperfect

The Outsiders

S.E. Hinton · 1967 · Required
Coming of Age
SAT Skills: Central Idea Tone & Voice Characterization Citing Evidence
Status
★★ ★★ ★
🎯Prompt 1 · Central IdeaSAT: Reading
Write a one-sentence central idea (theme) statement for this novel. It should go beyond the plot — state what the book says about life, people, or society. Then identify two pieces of textual evidence that support it.
💡 SAT Skill: "Central idea" questions are among the most common on SAT Reading. Practice writing: "The central idea is that ___" — then prove it with quotes.
🎙️Prompt 2 · Tone & DictionSAT: Language
Hinton wrote this at 16. Find three words or phrases that feel especially authentic to Ponyboy's voice. For each one: quote it, name the tone it creates (nostalgic? bitter? yearning?), and explain how the word choice affects meaning.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Writing section tests word choice — choosing the right word for tone and precision. Analyzing diction here trains that exact skill.
👥Prompt 3 · Contrasting PerspectivesSAT: Analysis
The SAT often presents two passages with different viewpoints. Practice here: how would a Soc narrator describe the rumble differently than Ponyboy? Write 3–4 sentences from the Soc perspective, then explain what changes and what stays true.
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-passage questions ask you to compare how two authors approach the same event. This exercise trains that exact thinking.
🎯SAT-Style Question
Johnny's dying message, "Stay gold," is best understood as which of the following?
A
A literal instruction to wear gold-colored clothing as a sign of gang loyalty
B
An allusion to a Frost poem urging Ponyboy to preserve his innocence and sensitivity
C
A warning that Ponyboy should avoid dangerous situations in the future
D
A reference to the gold sunset they once shared, suggesting Ponyboy should appreciate nature

The House on Mango Street

Sandra Cisneros · 1984 · Choice 3A
Vignette · Identity
SAT Skills: Author's Craft Structure & Form Imagery SAT Essay Writing
Status
★★ ★★ ★
🏗️Prompt 1 · Structure & EffectSAT: Reading
This book uses vignettes instead of chapters. The SAT asks how structure contributes to meaning. Choose one vignette — why is it short? What would be lost if it were a full chapter? What does the brevity itself communicate?
💡 SAT Skill: "How does the structure of the passage contribute to its meaning?" is a classic SAT question. This exercise builds direct practice.
🎨Prompt 2 · Imagery & ConnotationSAT: Language
Cisneros describes the house on Mango Street as "not the house we thought we'd get." Find two other images of houses, windows, or rooms in the book. What do they literally describe? What do they suggest beyond the literal (their connotation)?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT regularly asks about connotation — the feeling a word carries beyond its definition. Cisneros is a masterclass in this.
✍️Prompt 3 · Imitation WritingSAT: Writing
Write your own vignette (100–150 words) in Cisneros's style: present tense, sensory detail, a simple subject with a deeper meaning underneath. Then annotate it — label one image, one piece of sensory language, and one moment of deeper meaning.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Writing section tests your ability to produce precise, intentional sentences. Imitating a master author sharpens your own craft.
🎯SAT-Style Question
Which of the following best describes the function of Esperanza's repeated references to houses throughout the novel?
A
To provide realistic details about poverty in urban Chicago
B
To create a symbol for identity, belonging, and the tension between dreams and reality
C
To argue that housing policy must change to help immigrant families
D
To contrast Esperanza's neighborhood with wealthier parts of the city

Roll of Thunder, Hear My Cry

Mildred D. Taylor · 1976 · Choice 3B
Historical Fiction
SAT Skills: Historical Context Inference Argument & Evidence Paired Passages
Status
★★ ★★ ★
🔍Prompt 1 · Inference from EvidenceSAT: Inference
Cassie's mother is fired from her teaching job. Taylor never states directly why — the reader must infer it. Write out the inference: what happened, what details from the text lead you there, and what the author implies without stating outright.
💡 SAT Skill: Inference questions ("the passage most strongly suggests...") are on every SAT. Practice: claim → specific textual detail → logical conclusion.
📜Prompt 2 · Informational + Literary PairingSAT: Paired Passages
Find one fact from outside the novel — about sharecropping, the Jim Crow South, or the Great Depression — that deepens your understanding of a specific scene. Quote the historical fact, quote the scene, and explain how knowing the history changes the meaning.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT pairs literary and informational texts. This exercise trains you to synthesize across text types — a high-level skill.
⚡Prompt 3 · Argument AnalysisSAT: Analysis
David Logan argues that owning land is the only true freedom available to them. Is his argument convincing? Identify his claim, his evidence (from his actions and words), and evaluate the strength of his reasoning. Do you agree? Why or why not?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT Reading section includes argument passages. Practice identifying claim, evidence, and reasoning quality — then evaluating them critically.
🎯SAT-Style Question
When Cassie is forced to apologize to Lillian Jean, the author most likely includes this scene in order to:
A
Show that Cassie has genuinely learned to respect others regardless of race
B
Demonstrate how the social system forces Black individuals into public submission while preserving private dignity
C
Suggest that Cassie's mother gave her poor advice about how to handle conflict
D
Argue that racial tensions in the South were caused primarily by misunderstandings between children

Romeo and Juliet

William Shakespeare · c. 1597 · Choice C
Drama · Tragedy · Shakespeare
SAT Skills: Drama Structure Figurative Language Argument Language Precision
Status
★★ ★★ ★
🗡️Prompt 1 · Unpacking Shakespeare's LanguageSAT: Language
Choose the balcony scene (Act II, Scene II). Translate 8–10 lines into plain modern English. Then identify two moments where the poetic language — a metaphor, an image, the rhythm — says something your translation loses. What does this tell you about why Shakespeare wrote in verse instead of plain speech?
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT frequently includes Shakespeare or formal literary prose and tests whether you can identify what specific word choices accomplish. Translating first, then analyzing what gets lost, is the best training method.
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
⚖️Prompt 2 · Is Fate to Blame?SAT: Argument
Shakespeare calls Romeo and Juliet "star-crossed lovers" — fated to tragedy. But are they really victims of fate, or of their own choices? Build both arguments using evidence from the play: one case for fate, one for personal responsibility. Then decide which is stronger and explain why.
💡 SAT Skill: Building two opposing arguments from the same text, then evaluating which is better supported, is exactly what SAT argument questions require.
🔗Prompt 3 · Romeo & Juliet vs. The OutsidersSAT: Synthesis
Both Romeo and Juliet and The Outsiders center on young people trapped by conflict between groups they didn't choose. Compare how each author uses the conflict to explore the theme of loyalty. Quote one passage from each. Which author makes a more compelling case about what loyalty costs us?
💡 SAT Skill: Cross-text synthesis — connecting two works by theme and comparing how each handles it — is the SAT's highest-difficulty reading skill. This prompt is direct preparation.

The Watsons Go to Birmingham — 1963

Christopher Paul Curtis · 1995 · Choice D
Civil Rights · Family · History
SAT Skills: Tone & Humor Narrator Reliability Historical Context Paired Texts
Status
★★ ★★ ★
😄Prompt 1 · Humor as a Narrative ToolSAT: Tone
Curtis opens the novel with warmth and humor — Kenny narrates his family's quirks with affection and comic timing. Find two passages where the humor is strongest. For each: describe what makes the moment funny and what it reveals about the Watson family's bond. Then explain why Curtis chooses humor to open a novel that ends in tragedy. What does this tonal shift accomplish?
💡 SAT Skill: Tone — the emotional register an author establishes — is one of the SAT's most tested concepts. When a writer shifts from humor to grief, that contrast is deliberate and analytical gold. Always ask: why this tone, and why does it change?
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
🏛️Prompt 2 · The Child's Eye on HistorySAT: Narrator Analysis
Kenny is ten years old and doesn't fully understand what the civil rights movement is or what the Birmingham church bombing means. Find two moments where Kenny describes historical events through a child's limited understanding. For each: what does he see and report? What does the reader understand that Kenny doesn't? What does Curtis gain by using a child narrator for this particular story?
💡 SAT Skill: The gap between what a narrator understands and what the reader understands is the definition of dramatic irony — and a core SAT analysis concept. A child narrator makes this gap visible and powerful.
🔗Prompt 3 · The Watsons & To Kill a MockingbirdSAT: Paired Texts
Both The Watsons Go to Birmingham and To Kill a Mockingbird use a child narrator to tell a story about racial injustice in America. Compare Kenny Watson and Scout Finch: How does each child's age and position shape what they understand about the events around them? Quote one passage from each. What does reading these two child narrators side by side reveal about how literature uses innocence to tell hard truths?
💡 SAT Skill: Paired-text synthesis is the SAT's highest reading skill. Comparing two child narrators across different eras trains the precise analytical move the SAT rewards: same technique, different contexts, what does the comparison reveal?

Joey Pigza Swallowed the Key

Jack Gantos · 1998 · Choice E
ADHD · Empathy · Growing Up
SAT Skills: First-Person Narration Sympathy vs. Empathy Character Analysis Tone
Status
★★ ★★ ★
⚡Prompt 1 · Inside Joey's MindSAT: Narration
Gantos writes Joey's narration in a way that mimics how ADHD actually feels — fast, distracted, jumping between thoughts, intensely in the moment. Find two passages where the writing style itself reflects Joey's brain. Describe what the prose does differently from normal narration, and explain how this technique creates empathy in the reader.
💡 SAT Skill: Narrative style — how a story is told, not just what happens — is a core SAT analytical concept. When form mirrors content (the writing feels like the character thinks), that's deliberate craft.
📌 Cite the Text — SAT: Evidence
🏫Prompt 2 · Systems & KidsSAT: Argument
Joey struggles not just with ADHD but with systems — school, family, special education — that were not built with him in mind. Identify two moments where a system fails Joey. For each: what was the system supposed to do? What did it actually do? What is Gantos arguing about how institutions treat kids who don't fit the mold?
💡 SAT Skill: Identifying an author's implicit argument — the claim embedded in the story rather than stated outright — is one of the SAT's most tested inference skills.
🔗Prompt 3 · Joey & Holden / Joey & StarrSAT: Synthesis
Compare Joey to one other narrator from this year's reading list. What do they share? How are their challenges different? Which narrator do you find more reliable — meaning, which one do you trust to tell their own story accurately — and why? Use a quote from each book.
💡 SAT Skill: Narrator reliability is a high-frequency SAT concept. When a narrator has limited self-awareness, the reader must read between the lines — exactly what this comparison trains.

Independent Reading

Caged Bird Sings · Ender's Game · or your approved title
Independent Choice
SAT Skills: Synthesis Argument All Reading Skills Comparative Analysis
My Book:
Status
★★ ★★ ★
🎯Prompt 1 · Claim + EvidenceSAT: Evidence
Make an argument about your book's central theme in one sentence. Then provide three pieces of textual evidence — each a direct quote — that support your claim. For each quote, write one sentence explaining the connection.
💡 SAT Skill: This is the foundational SAT writing move: Claim → Evidence → Explanation (CEE). Every SAT essay and evidence question uses this structure.
🌱Prompt 2 · Character ArcSAT: Analysis
Describe the main character's arc: who are they at the start vs. the end? Identify the single most important turning point — the moment where change becomes inevitable. Quote the passage where that shift is clearest, and explain what makes it the pivot.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT asks "what does this passage suggest about the character?" Practice locating the precise moment of change — not summarizing the whole arc.
🔗Prompt 3 · Cross-Text SynthesisSAT: Synthesis
Compare your independent book to one other book from this list. Identify one shared theme and one key difference in how each author develops it. Use a quote from each book. Which treatment do you find more convincing, and why?
💡 SAT Skill: Synthesis — drawing connections across texts — is the highest-order SAT reading skill. This prompt directly practices it.
🖊️Prompt 4 · Practice Short EssaySAT: Writing
Write a focused 3-paragraph response: (1) Introduce the book and state your claim about its theme. (2) Develop the claim with a quote and analysis. (3) Conclude by explaining why this theme matters beyond the book. Aim for 200–250 words. This is SAT essay practice.
💡 SAT Skill: The SAT essay (where included) rewards clear thesis, specific evidence, and logical development. This is full practice.
